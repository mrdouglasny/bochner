> For a continuous integrable positive-definite function $\psi$, the integral has nonnegative real part: $\Re(\int \psi) \geq 0$.

### Main Theorem

[`pd_l1_fourier_re_nonneg_theorem`](../../Bochner/FejerPD.lean#576)
> **Theorem**: For a continuous integrable positive-definite function $\varphi : V \to \mathbb{C}$ on a finite-dimensional real inner product space, the Fourier transform has nonnegative real part for all $\xi \in V$:
> $$0 \leq \Re(\mathcal{F}\varphi(\xi))$$

**Proof outline**:
1. Rewrite Fourier integral: $\mathcal{F}\varphi(\xi) = \int_V \varphi(v) \cdot e^{-2\pi i \langle v, \xi \rangle} \, dv$.
2. Recognize the integrand as $\psi(v) = \varphi(v) \cdot e^{2\pi i \langle v, -\xi \rangle}$, which is positive-definite (Schur product of $\varphi$ with the PD exponential character).
3. Verify $\psi$ is continuous and integrable (norm of exponential is 1).
4. Apply `pd_integral_re_nonneg` to conclude.

Proof uses: `isPositiveDefinite_exp_inner`, `IsPositiveDefinite.mul` (Schur product), `Complex.continuous_exp`, `Integrable.mono`, `norm_exp_ofReal_mul_I`, `pd_integral_re_nonneg`.
